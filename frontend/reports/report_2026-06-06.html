SECTION 4 — Why Markets Moved

Friday June 5 was a brutal end to the week for US tech. The Nasdaq Composite fell 4.18% — its biggest single-day drop since the tariff turmoil of April 2025 — with the Nasdaq 100 (QQQ) closing down 4.8% to 705.06. Semiconductors led the carnage: Micron (-13.25%), RGTI (-14.4%), AMD (-10.86%), AVGO (-7.92%), NVDA (-6.2%), and GFS (-10.83%) all suffered double-digit or near-double-digit losses on the day. The broader S&P 500 (SPY) fell 2.58%, while defensive sectors — Consumer Staples (+1.71%), Utilities (+0.93%), and Real Estate (+0.68%) — attracted safe-haven flows.

Two catalysts collided. First, Broadcom (AVGO) reported earnings Wednesday night and failed to raise its AI chip revenue outlook sufficiently, triggering a two-day selloff across the entire semiconductor complex. Second, and more forcefully, Friday morning's May non-farm payrolls report showed 172,000 new jobs — roughly double the consensus estimate of 85,000 — fuelling 'good news is bad news' dynamics. The 10-year Treasury yield spiked to 4.54% and the 30-year topped 5%, raising the spectre that the Federal Reserve might delay or even reverse its easing cycle. Rate-hike probability rose sharply, weighing most heavily on high-multiple growth and AI-related names. A reported Meta secondary share offering further compressed sentiment. Notably, some economists attributed part of the jobs surge to early World Cup hiring — concentrated in leisure & hospitality (+70k) — suggesting the core labour market may be somewhat softer than the headline implies.

For Singapore-based retail investors, the selloff has created a complex but potentially opportunity-rich landscape heading into next week. The VIX (15.4), though it reportedly spiked intraday above 20 before settling, remains in 'calm' territory on a closing basis, and the S&P 500 sits +3.4% above its 50-day moving average — both suggesting the sell-off is a sharp but contained pullback rather than the start of a sustained bear move. The critical macro events ahead are May CPI (June 10), May PPI (June 11), and — most importantly — the June 16-17 FOMC meeting where the Fed will update its dot-plot projections. Until then, semiconductor stocks may struggle to regain ground, while defensive and value-oriented sectors offer relative shelter. Disciplined investors with long horizons should view post-selloff discounts on quality names carefully, but should not chase beaten-down high-beta chips in the immediate near-term.
